When I was six or seven years old, Hasbro ran a contest to name the next Care Bear. The prize was a Commodore 64. I entered. I won. I named Tender Heart the Bear.

My daughters thought that was the greatest thing in the world when they were little. They'd dress up as Tender Heart for Halloween. For me, it was the beginning of everything.

---

This was around 1982 or 1983. There was no Windows. There were cartridge-based games: Atari, the arcade version of Pac-Man if you were lucky. But the Commodore was something different. It didn't come with instructions that said "here's how to play." It came with a keyboard and a blank screen and the expectation that you'd figure out the rest.

So I figured things out. I started writing my own text-based role-playing games. Simple stuff by any modern standard, but back then there was no saving your work to a disk. If I turned the machine off, I lost everything. So I left it on for weeks at a time, living inside this little world I'd built, adding to it whenever I could.

I didn't fully grasp at the time what I was actually building in myself: patience with hard problems, the habit of thinking through things step by step, the ability to sit with something that didn't give you quick answers. I just thought I was having fun.

---

My mom worked hard. She worked nights, she worked days, she did what she had to do. The message she drilled into me from a young age was simple: you get off your butt and you work, and you treat people right. On my 15th birthday, I got a job sacking groceries at Minyard's in Lakewood (now a Whole Foods, if you want a measure of how long ago that was). When I turned 16 and the child labor restrictions loosened up, I moved to McDonald's for more hours and better pay. Within two years I was crew trainer, head of the safety committee, and I'd memorized the prep procedure for every item on the menu. Nobody else at that location had done it. I got a special award for it. I still have it.

I tell that story not to be nostalgic about fast food, but because it illustrates something I believed even then: whatever you're doing, do it completely. Be the one person in the room who actually showed up all the way.

High school, I'll be honest, was not my finest stretch. I had three freshman years. The last one, I had four and a half credits when I needed five to advance. I ended up skipping my sophomore year entirely, was junior class president, did well my senior year, and graduated. My GPA was never going to open doors on its own, so I understood early I'd need to make my own.

---

The break came through the printing industry. I started working nights, 10pm to 7am, and someone there took a chance on me. The printing world was in the middle of a massive transition, converting from Exacto knives and ruby lith film to digital production. Nobody had a roadmap. They just needed people who could learn fast.

Because of that Commodore from years earlier, I picked it up quickly. One opportunity led to another, the way they do when you become the person people can turn to. They sent me to California for SGI-IRIX training (Silicon Graphics, the machines behind CG film effects in the '90s). I worked at that company for 11 years and kept getting promoted. I also met my wife there in 1997. We've been together 28 years now.

She's a developer, and when the company made clear they weren't going to move her into programming, she left in 2000 for a web and digital services shop that would actually let her do the work. Five years later, after enough Christmas parties and enough people telling me I should make the jump, I followed her. On my first day, someone told me not to talk to the developers because they'd ask me to do things.

My wife was a developer. I ignored that advice immediately.

---

What I found on the other side of that wall was the business I'd spend the next decade building. The gap I saw was in what I started calling "Content Logistics," the messy, unglamorous space between the marketing agencies that create content and the technical service providers who host it. Nobody owned that space. When a project went sideways, when you were over budget, behind schedule, with three new people who'd replaced the three who'd quit and every vendor pointing at every other vendor, there was no one whose job it was to actually fix it.

I thought that sounded like a tremendous opportunity.

I started charging clients for it. The owners let me run with it. What had started as something informal grew into a multi-million dollar, high-margin business unit. And the whole time, I kept coming back to the same feeling that had hooked me on that Commodore as a kid: there is nothing quite like when something finally works.

Tech is unusual that way. Most jobs don't give you many wins you can feel. Birthdays, graduations, the occasional raise. But in technology, when you really understand what goes into it, you get small wins constantly. Something compiles. A problem you've been staring at for three days resolves itself at 11pm on a Tuesday. You send a link to a junior engineer, count down from five, and hear them go "Oh my God" when it clicks. Those moments add up. They keep you hungry.

---

The thing I've spent the most time thinking about isn't the technology itself. It's the bigger question underneath it: why most people don't try.

Someone told me once, and I don't remember who, that somebody has to build rockets. Somebody has to do neurosurgery. Somebody has to invent the next thing. The people doing it today are going to retire. The world keeps growing. And when you're young, you look at all of that and think those people already exist, that world is already full.

But it isn't. It never is.

What stops most people isn't lack of talent. It's that they've quietly decided the answer to "why not me?" is something they'd rather not hear. It's easier not to try than to try and find out. So they don't climb the ladder at all, or they lean it against whatever building is closest rather than the one they actually want to reach.

I got a Commodore 64 because a stuffed bear needed a name. What I did with it after that was a choice.

Every kid at six has something that lights them up. The question worth asking, the one I've spent years trying to help people answer, is what you've done with yours.
