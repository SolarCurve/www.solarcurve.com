When I was six or seven, I won a Commodore 64. Can't remember exactly how, and neither can my mom.

I learned BASIC and started building text-based games. The problem was I couldn't save them. No disk drive meant the machine stayed on for weeks at a time to keep anything I'd built alive. Eventually the power supply fried itself. Then the whole thing started overheating.

My dad was a NASA and Air Force engineer. We sat down together and designed an external power supply. Then we built a custom case to hold everything: the new disk drive, the joysticks, space for the TV on top. It was a proper engineering project, sized and measured before anything got cut.

I still have that case. The Commodore 64 is gone, but I can't let go of what we built together. My dad died in 2011. That case is one of the few physical things I have left from our time working side by side.

I've been thinking about that project a lot lately. Maybe that's where my obsession with power infrastructure actually started. A failing power supply forced me to understand a constraint and engineer around it. Before I knew what engineering was, I was doing it — with a NASA engineer guiding every measurement.

The tools change. The problem never does. You still have to understand what's actually limiting a system before you can build something that lasts inside of it.
