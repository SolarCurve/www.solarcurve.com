When I was six or seven, I won a Commodore 64. Can't remember exactly how, and neither can my mom.

What I do remember: we plugged it in, the power supply failed almost immediately, and instead of returning it, my dad decided we were going to fix it. My dad was a NASA engineer for the Air Force. He had opinions about how things should work.

He also had opinions about how they should be housed.

The Commodore came in a beige plastic case that he found unacceptable. So we designed a new one. Wood, custom dimensions, fitted compartments for the components. I was a kid and he was the engineer, but he walked me through every decision. Why this wood. Why these tolerances. Why you measure twice before you cut anything.

We built it together. It took weeks. When we finished, we had a working C64 in a case that looked like furniture.

That machine is what made me want to understand how computers work. Not just use them — understand them. The combination of hardware and software, the way a physical system could run logical processes, the way you could build something that didn't exist yet out of components that did.

My dad died in 2011. I still have the case we built.

I think about that project a lot when I'm dealing with infrastructure problems — the ones where the right answer isn't buying something off the shelf, it's building what you actually need. That instinct didn't come from a computer science degree. It came from a wood shop and a broken power supply and a father who believed that if something was worth doing, it was worth doing right.

The machine changed everything. The case is why.
