In the 2000s, before Slack, Chef, PagerDuty, Splunk, and AWS existed, we were already building our own versions.

We didn't call it DevOps. We just did the work.

An IRC server with bots for team communication, because email was too slow and nothing else existed. Configuration management tools we wrote ourselves before Puppet or Ansible shipped. Automated monitoring and paging by wiring WhatsUp into Skype. Deployment pipelines built from whatever scripts we could hack together between environments.

The webcam pointed at a grid of RSA tokens so we didn't have to carry them everywhere? That was before authenticator apps existed. We built what we needed because what we needed didn't exist yet.

We were competing with billion-dollar platforms using duct tape and imagination.

The tools got better. The problems stayed the same. You still have to build what doesn't exist yet, whether that's with PHP scripts in 2003 or a billion-dollar SaaS platform in 2024. The constraint was never the tools. It was whether you were willing to solve the problem with what you had — and whether you understood the problem well enough to know what "solved" actually meant.

I think about those years a lot when I see teams waiting for the perfect tool, the perfect stack, the right moment. That moment rarely comes on its own. You build your way to it.
