Remember when we thought Counter-Strike would never get old? Turns out, we were part of something way bigger than we realized at the time. Here's what your kids (and maybe your friends who weren't gaming back then) need to know about why the early 2000s were actually insane.

## Dallas was the center of the gaming universe

This isn't hyperbole — Dallas *literally was* where professional gaming got invented. The Cyberathlete Professional League (CPL) started here in 1997, and they held major tournaments twice a year at the Hyatt Regency. By December 2001, the CPL Winter tournament had a $150,000 prize pool — think about that for a second. In 2001, gaming tournaments were giving away amounts of money that rivaled actual sports events. The first Counter-Strike "major" ever held? December 2001, Dallas. That's where the blueprint for competitive CS got written.

Fast forward a few years, and North American teams were absolutely dominating the international scene. In 18 major tournaments between 2002 and 2007, NA teams placed top-3 at 14 of them. That's not luck — that was infrastructure, practice, and organization. And a huge part of that infrastructure was built by people like us in Texas.

## What TAU actually was

Texas Area Untouchables wasn't just a team I founded and ran — TAU was competing at CAL-Invite level, which was legitimately "the NBA of esports" back then. The Cyberathlete Amateur League had about 20,000 registered teams at its peak, split into different divisions. Making it to CAL-I meant our roster (Beavis007, $hield, HeavyArms, ScubaSteve, and the others) were in the top few hundred best players in North America. We were *that* good.

And it wasn't just online play — TAU showed up to CPL LANs and held their own. We beat Team NoA, an international powerhouse that traveled to compete in North America. When HeavyArms faced Team 3D in 2002, TAU was stepping into the cage with the teams that would define the era. Team 3D went on to win the CPL Winter 2002 championship and took home $30,000. We were competing for that same money, on the same stage.

## The cafes and events were the real game

Here's the part that actually mattered more than people realized: we didn't just play — we *built the scene*. The Gaming Center (TGC) gaming cafes I owned and managed in the DFW area? Those were essential infrastructure. Before everyone had high-speed internet at home, gaming cafes were where the competitive scene lived. They provided the PCs, the bandwidth, and most importantly, the gathering space where players could practice, compete locally, and develop the skills to move up to CAL and CPL. We ran TANGO LAN events that fed that pipeline — players cut their teeth at our events, then moved up to bigger tournaments. That's community building, and it mattered.

Then we sponsored Natural Selection and got it a spot at CPL LANs. Natural Selection was a groundbreaking Half-Life mod that blended first-person shooter mechanics with real-time strategy gameplay — it came out October 31, 2002. It was ambitious enough that it would later be called "possibly the most ambitious user-made modification ever brought to fruition." Our decision to sponsor it and support its competitive scene meant we were actively growing esports *beyond* just Counter-Strike. That's the mark of someone who wasn't just chasing the game — we were trying to grow the whole ecosystem.

## Why this still matters

When your kids are watching esports in 2026, they're watching the direct descendant of what we built. The prize pools, the league structures, the pathway from local competitions to professional play, the concept of gaming cafes and community hubs — all of that DNA came from the 2000s. We were there when esports went from "those nerds in my basement" to "legitimate competition with real money and international audiences."

The squad that played TAU with us back then? We're all still gaming together, and we built something in those years that's still part of gaming culture today. Our kids should know that.
