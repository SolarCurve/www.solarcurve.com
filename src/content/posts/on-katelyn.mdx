I've posted a fair amount about my youngest, so today is about my oldest, Katelyn.

Age 8: she started "Art Madness," selling custom art and t-shirts to her classmates. Age 10: reading chemical engineering books and writing code on the computer. Also at 10: delivered a keynote speech at a tech conference. High school: robotics competitions and a string of STEM achievements that I watched with equal parts pride and mild astonishment.

![Katelyn's keynote](/images/kk-keynote.jpg)

![Katelyn in robotics](/images/kk-robotics.jpg)

![Katelyn at a TSA conference in high school](/images/kk-tsa.jpg)

Currently: RA of the Year at Texas State University. President of the McCoy School of Business Ambassadors. Has traveled to 18 countries.

![Katelyn, RA of the Year](/images/kk-ra-of-the-year.jpg)

![Katelyn's Texas State Homecoming Queen campaign poster](/images/kk-homecoming-queen.jpg)

The pattern I've watched across all of it: when you step up, show up, and give real effort, opportunities appear. Those opportunities create bigger opportunities. It compounds.

Gladwell describes it in Outliers — 10,000 hours at anything makes you an expert. What the book doesn't fully capture is that the hours have to be intentional. You have to be doing the thing, not just near it. Katelyn has always been doing the thing.

Watching your kids develop their own paths and their own work ethic is one of the unexpected rewards of parenthood. Watching them become genuinely expert at something — not because you pushed them there but because they drove themselves there — is a reward I didn't know to expect.
