Back in January I wrote about the [three themes I'm carrying into 2026](/posts/three-themes-carrying-into-2026/) — distributed teams, the gig economy blurring who owns what, and AI landing squarely in the middle of knowledge work. I thought I was just reflecting in an empty-nester rabbit hole. Turns out I was taking notes for a book.

Here's the part I usually don't lead with on this blog. Alongside the solar panels and the SSD benchmarks, I spend my working life in client-facing leadership — seven companies now, most of them built on account service. And roughly a decade ago, in a workshop at a digital agency called imc², I met two psychologists-turned-business-consultants, Dr. Mark McKinney and Dr. Steve Fedorko. I sat in the front row taking notes like the answer was going to be on a test.

Their book, *My Client is the Devil (And Other Myths)*, lived on my nightstand for years. I assigned pages to my teams. I put it on a slide in a training program I built called "The Relentless Pursuit of Excellence," listed as required reading without apology. I kept shipping copies to people right up through my seventh company.

What changed wasn't my belief in the framework. It was the world the framework had to operate in. Remote work went from fringe to default almost overnight. The workforce went global in a daily, practical sense. The gig economy scrambled accountability and loyalty. And then AI showed up — not as a future consideration but as a present-tense disruptor. All of it created more invisible work and more unnamed pressure, arriving from directions the 2015 edition never could have seen coming.

So last summer I called Mark. I didn't come empty-handed — I brought a decade of notes, updated examples, and enough familiarity with the original to know where it bent under the current moment and where it held. Mark and Steve had the generosity to invite me into the work itself.

The result is a revisited edition of **[My Client is the Devil (And Other Myths)](https://www.myclientisthedevil.com/)**, now co-authored by the three of us. It keeps the original framework and names the new forces bearing down on anyone responsible for clients, teams, and outcomes they don't fully control. Its take on the AI question is one I've quoted here before in spirit: your job is to think, and AI's job is to help you think better.

Everyone has clients. Everyone wears the Account Lead hat for someone. If you're responsible to another person for outcomes — regardless of your title — it was written for you.

There's a [free sampler of 15 myths](https://www.myclientisthedevil.com/myths-sampler/) from the new edition if you want an early look. And if you've ever caught yourself thinking the client is the problem, well — that's kind of the whole point of the title.
