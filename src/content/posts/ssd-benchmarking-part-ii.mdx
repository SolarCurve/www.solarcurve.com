So it's been a whole 30 minutes since I published [Part I](/posts/ssd-benchmarking/) and I already have the answers I was seeking. As many fellow bloggers know, the act of writing something down, thinking it through, and revisiting it even a few minutes later grants great clarity.

After posting and tweeting, I had the idea that the bus speed of the SATA 3 controller card might not be enough. The market on Amazon and NewEgg is littered with controllers that are PCI Express 1x. I started looking at posts and speeds on this port — and sure enough, that's what my new drive was topping out at.

The onboard SATA 2 controller didn't have that limitation, so it was able to go faster. My options: find a controller card that's more than 1x so it has more bandwidth, or update my motherboard to one with native SATA 3 onboard. If I'm going to do that, I might as well upgrade everything. Given that I'm an every-three-years upgrade kind of guy, I'm going to sit tight and wait until this time next year.

Standards are always changing and technology is always evolving. I'm patient enough with my money to know I'll have more options and lower costs if I wait. For now, I'm going to appreciate my WEI disk score going from **5.9 to 7.8** — that's a pretty huge leap.

## The slot situation

I went down the road of reviewing how many PCI Express slots my motherboard has. While there are three PCI Express 2.0 slots, they operate in modes of 16x/16x/0, 16x/0x/16 (my configuration), or 16x/8x/8. This means that if I wanted to tap into that third slot, I'd hurt my video card performance. While I don't game across all four monitors at once, I do want to keep them all optimal — I occasionally flip on SLI and 3D.

The SATA 3 controller cards that would fit were priced from $200 to $800. That's too much even for this enthusiast.

I wish I had better news to report but it is what it is. In hindsight, I could have saved $30 by getting the non-GT version of the drive and not worrying about SATA 3 at all. I wish drive manufacturers released more real-world information — but I also understand why they don't.

## Bonus fix

I didn't know that total bandwidth among PCI Express 2.0 ports was limited and you couldn't run each slot at full 16x simultaneously. I had an eSATA controller card in my middle slot that was robbing one of my video cards and keeping it at 8x instead of 16x. I swapped it into the 1x slot I vacated by removing the SATA 3 card. Both video cards are now back to full speed.

Always worth auditing what's in your expansion slots.
