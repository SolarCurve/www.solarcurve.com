When my dad and I decided to build a custom case for our Commodore 64, the first thing he did was sit me down with graph paper.

Not a saw. Not wood. Graph paper.

We spent hours drawing plans before we touched a single material. He was a NASA engineer for the Air Force, and he approached the case the way he approached everything: you don't build until you know exactly what you're building. Dimensions. Tolerances. Component placement. Ventilation. The plan had to answer every question before the work started.

I was seven or eight. I wanted to start cutting. He made me measure.

"The answers are in the measurements, not the assumptions." He said something like that. I've carried it for thirty years.

I still have some of those original planning diagrams. Looking at them now, I'm struck by how seriously he took it — a custom case for a kid's computer, planned with the same rigor he'd apply to anything else. He didn't have a lower standard for things that were "just" a hobby project. The standard was the standard.

That's what I absorbed without realizing I was absorbing it. You measure first. You plan before you build. You answer the questions on paper so you don't answer them expensively in wood, or in production, or after the thing has already shipped.

I've worked in infrastructure for thirty years. The failures I remember most clearly are almost always failures of measurement — assumptions that were never tested, questions that were never asked, gaps between what we thought we were building and what we were actually building.

Measure first. Then cut.
