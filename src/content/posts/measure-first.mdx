After I shared the story of the Commodore 64 my dad and I built together, a few people asked about the actual build process.

I went looking and found the planning diagrams we drew up. There's a photo of me using a ruler at the chalkboard. He made me measure everything first. That wasn't a suggestion.

Look at the schematic with all the labels — remote switch box, power supply, disk drive, bargraph. The measurements are in 30-inch increments. We had to know whether the whole thing would fit under the TV before anyone cut a single piece of wood.

One drawing looked off to me. The proportions seemed wrong. My dad checked the numbers and told me to leave it. The math was correct, even if it didn't look correct.

That stayed with me: the answers are in the measurements, not the assumptions.

What's not visible in the photos is that we started with the power sled to validate the design. Before anything else got built, we had to know the power mechanisms worked. Only once the PSU was validated did we start commissioning the rest of the cabinet.

In every infrastructure project I've touched since then, the same principle has held. You can design the most elegant space in the world, but without the right power in place, nothing turns on. Power first. Everything else follows.

The teams that consistently deliver don't just plan the work. They measure the constraints — timelines, lead times, capacity limits, redundancy requirements — and design the program around those realities before committing to the build.

My dad taught me that before I was ten years old, in our living room, with a ruler and a chalkboard.
