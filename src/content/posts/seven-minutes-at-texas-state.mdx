My daughter Katelyn is President of the McCoy School of Business Ambassadors at Texas State University. She asked me last minute to speak to the group about my career. Elite students, high achievers. Seven minutes.

High school, no college. That was the starting point.

I walked them through each step up — what I learned at each level, how soft skills ended up mattering as much as technical ones. They asked about imposter syndrome. They asked about certifications. I told them about Toastmasters.

Twenty minutes in, I was still answering questions. Students wanted to know how you actually build a career when you don't have the traditional credentials everyone seems to expect.

Here's what I should have said but ran out of time: your career path doesn't have to look like anyone else's. Each role teaches you something. String enough of those together with real effort behind each one, and you've built expertise nobody else has — because nobody else took that specific path.

The Toastmasters investment paid off that night. You can't fake your way through seven minutes in front of people who know when you're full of it.

What surprised me most was how many of those students — accomplished, clearly smart, already ahead of most — were carrying around imposter syndrome like it was standard issue. It isn't. It's a signal. It means you care and you're paying attention. The people who never feel it usually aren't doing anything hard enough to matter.

The advice I give myself is the advice I gave them: keep climbing, tell the truth about where you started, and trust that the path makes sense in hindsight even when it doesn't in the moment.
