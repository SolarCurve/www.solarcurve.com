To many people I'm just a nerd, and most of them don't know what an SSD drive is or why they're taking over. In short, Solid State Drives are a much faster technology with no moving parts — used in iPods, smartphones, tablets, and increasingly desktop PCs. They're more expensive and smaller, but the speed difference is real.

My goal: take my Windows Experience Index (WEI) disk score from **5.9 to as high as possible**.

**New drive:** [Corsair Force 3 GT 120GB SATA 3](http://www.corsair.com/ssd/force-series-gt-ssd/force-series-gt-120gb-sata-3-6gbps-solid-state-hard-drive.html)

Why this drive: best performance-per-dollar on NewEgg, a "Fastest/Best Performance" award, and honestly — it's red, and it matched my red Corsair Dominator memory. I'm a builder. These things matter.

## Step 1: Enable AHCI mode

This is the most important step. AHCI (Advanced Host Controller Interface) is designed for faster storage and gets past limitations of the legacy IDE interface. Do this *before* installing Windows on the new drive — if you try to enable it after the fact, you'll blue screen.

The fix: in the registry at `HKLM\SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Services\msahci`, set `Start` to `0`. If you have an Intel chip, do the same for `iaStorV`. Reboot into BIOS and switch to AHCI mode, then let Windows reinstall the driver on first boot.

## Step 2: Enable TRIM

TRIM is a command set created specifically for SSDs that handles deletions and garbage collection differently from spinning drives. SSDs have a finite number of writes — TRIM extends their life and maintains performance. On Windows 7 with a modern drive it's usually auto-enabled, but verify:

```cmd
fsutil behavior query DisableDeleteNotify
```

`DisableDeleteNotify = 0` means TRIM is on. If it shows `1`, enable it:

```cmd
fsutil behavior set DisableDeleteNotify 0
```

That third result was the revelation. A SATA 2 controller was outperforming a SATA 3 card. Why?

The PCI Express 1x slot that the controller card was in only has the bandwidth of PCI-e 1.0 × 1. That's roughly 250 MB/s theoretical, which the SATA 3 drive immediately saturates. The onboard SATA 2 controller had a direct path to the chipset with more effective bandwidth, so it actually got better real-world throughput.

**WEI disk score result: 5.9 → 7.8 out of 7.9.**

Not quite 7.9, but the ceiling was the PCI-e slot bandwidth, not the drive. To squeeze out the last 0.1, I'd need a SATA 3 controller on a PCI-e 4x or 8x slot, or a newer motherboard with native SATA 3 onboard. Given that I'm an "upgrade every 3 years" kind of person, I'm happy to sit at 7.8 and wait.

---

**Bonus discovery**: I had an eSATA controller card in my second PCI-e slot that was silently running one of my video cards at 8x instead of 16x. Moved it to the 1x slot I vacated and got both video cards back to full speed. Always worth auditing what's in your expansion slots.
