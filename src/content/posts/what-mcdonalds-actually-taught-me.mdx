At 16, I worked at McDonald's. Started making buns. Got promoted to the grill. Then food assembly. I was obsessed with speed and accuracy, especially on custom orders. Feeding people in a hurry felt important.

That's when they offered me a Crew Trainer role for a quarter raise. Then the Safety Committee for another bump. I noticed something: every role had more to unlock if you were willing to learn it all the way through.

So I did. McDonald's had a Standard Operating Procedure for everything — making a Chef Salad, cleaning the grills at close, opening procedures, every station on every shift. I studied every single one. Passed all the tests. Got a dollar raise and a custom spatula with the store name engraved on it.

When I asked how many other employees had done the same, the answer was zero. Not because it was hard or secret. Most people were doing what they needed to do to get through the shift, which is completely understandable. But I wanted to know what was actually there.

"The difference between ordinary and extraordinary is that little extra." — that's Jimmy Johnson, and I'd heard it, but at 16 I started to feel what it meant in practice.

That extra quarter at 16 turned into a pattern I've carried through every role since. Wherever I've landed, I've looked for what was there to learn, what was there to solve, what was there that nobody else had gotten to yet. Going beyond the baseline consistently created opportunities I didn't see coming.

You don't have to be the most talented person in the room. You just have to be willing to go further into the job than everyone else thought was necessary.
